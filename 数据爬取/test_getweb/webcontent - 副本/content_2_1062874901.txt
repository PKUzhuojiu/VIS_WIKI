<p><b>Data visualization</b> is an interdisciplinary field that deals with the <a href="/wiki/Graphics" title="Graphics">graphic</a> <a href="/wiki/Representation_(arts)" title="Representation (arts)">representation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Data" title="Data">data</a>. It is a particularly efficient way of communicating when the data is numerous as for example a <a href="/wiki/Time_series" title="Time series">time series</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nussbaumer_Knaflic_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nussbaumer_Knaflic-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>From an academic point of view, this representation can be considered as a mapping between the original data (usually numerical) and graphic elements<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> (for example, lines or points in a chart). The mapping determines how the attributes of these elements vary according to the data. In this light, a bar chart is a mapping of the length of a bar to a magnitude of a variable. Since the graphic design of the mapping can adversely affect the readability of a chart,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nussbaumer_Knaflic_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nussbaumer_Knaflic-1">[1]</a></sup> mapping is a core competency of Data visualization.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gershon_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gershon-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Data visualization has its roots in the field of <a href="/wiki/Statistics" title="Statistics">Statistics</a> and is therefore generally considered a branch of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Descriptive_Statistics" title="Descriptive Statistics">Descriptive Statistics</a>. However, because both design skills and statistical and computing skills are required to visualize effectively, it is argued by some authors that it is both an Art and a Science.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gershon_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gershon-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Research into how people read and misread various types of visualizations is helping to determine what types and features of visualizations are most understandable and effective in conveying information.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mason_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mason-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-O'Donoghue_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-O'Donoghue-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:332px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Data_visualization_process_v1.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="720" data-file-width="960" decoding="async" height="248" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Data_visualization_process_v1.png/330px-Data_visualization_process_v1.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Data_visualization_process_v1.png/495px-Data_visualization_process_v1.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Data_visualization_process_v1.png/660px-Data_visualization_process_v1.png 2x" width="330"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Data_visualization_process_v1.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Data visualization is one of the steps in analyzing data and presenting it to users.</div></div></div>
<p>To communicate information clearly and efficiently, data visualization uses <a href="/wiki/Statistical_graphics" title="Statistical graphics">statistical graphics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Plot_(graphics)" title="Plot (graphics)">plots</a>, <a href="/wiki/Infographic" title="Infographic">information graphics</a> and other tools. Numerical data may be encoded using dots, lines, or bars, to visually communicate a quantitative message.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-6">[6]</a></sup> Effective visualization helps users analyze and reason about data and evidence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> It makes complex data more accessible, understandable, and usable. Users may have particular analytical tasks, such as making comparisons or understanding <a href="/wiki/Causality" title="Causality">causality</a>, and the design principle of the graphic (i.e., showing comparisons or showing causality) follows the task. Tables are generally used where users will look up a specific measurement, while charts of various types are used to show patterns or relationships in the data for one or more variables.
</p><p>Data visualization refers to the techniques used to communicate data or information by encoding it as visual objects (e.g., points, lines, or bars) contained in graphics. The goal is to communicate information clearly and efficiently to users. It is one of the steps in <a href="/wiki/Data_analysis" title="Data analysis">data analysis</a> or <a href="/wiki/Data_science" title="Data science">data science</a>. According to Vitaly Friedman (2008) the "main goal of data visualization is to communicate information clearly and effectively through graphical means. It doesn't mean that data visualization needs to look boring to be functional or extremely sophisticated to look beautiful. To convey ideas effectively, both aesthetic form and functionality need to go hand in hand, providing insights into a rather sparse and complex data set by communicating its key aspects in a more intuitive way. Yet designers often fail to achieve a balance between form and function, creating gorgeous data visualizations which fail to serve their main purpose — to communicate information".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>Indeed, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fernanda_Viegas" title="Fernanda Viegas">Fernanda Viegas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Martin_M._Wattenberg" title="Martin M. Wattenberg">Martin M. Wattenberg</a> suggested that an ideal visualization should not only communicate clearly, but stimulate viewer engagement and attention.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>Data visualization is closely related to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Information_graphics" title="Information graphics">information graphics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Information_visualization" title="Information visualization">information visualization</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scientific_visualization" title="Scientific visualization">scientific visualization</a>, <a href="/wiki/Exploratory_data_analysis" title="Exploratory data analysis">exploratory data analysis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Statistical_graphics" title="Statistical graphics">statistical graphics</a>. In the new millennium, data visualization has become an active area of research, teaching and development. According to Post et al. (2002), it has united scientific and information visualization.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FHP02_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-FHP02-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the commercial environment data visualization is often referred to as <a href="/wiki/Dashboard_(business)" title="Dashboard (business)">dashboards</a>. <a href="/wiki/Infographic" title="Infographic">Infographics</a> are another very common form of data visualization.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Underpinnings">Underpinnings</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics_of_effective_graphical_displays">Characteristics of effective graphical displays</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:442px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Minard.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="955" data-file-width="2003" decoding="async" height="210" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Minard.png/440px-Minard.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Minard.png/660px-Minard.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Minard.png/880px-Minard.png 2x" width="440"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Minard.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Charles_Joseph_Minard" title="Charles Joseph Minard">Charles Joseph Minard</a>'s 1869 diagram of <a href="/wiki/French_invasion_of_Russia" title="French invasion of Russia">Napoleonic France's invasion of Russia</a>, an early example of an information graphic</div></div></div>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1062260506">.mw-parser-output .quotebox{background-color:#F9F9F9;border:1px solid #aaa;box-sizing:border-box;padding:10px;font-size:88%;max-width:100%}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatleft{margin:.5em 1.4em .8em 0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatright{margin:.5em 0 .8em 1.4em}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.centered{overflow:hidden;position:relative;margin:.5em auto .8em auto}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatleft span,.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatright span{font-style:inherit}.mw-parser-output .quotebox>blockquote{margin:0;padding:0;border-left:0;font-family:inherit;font-size:inherit}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-title{background-color:#F9F9F9;text-align:center;font-size:110%;font-weight:bold}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-quote>:first-child{margin-top:0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-quote:last-child>:last-child{margin-bottom:0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-quote.quoted:before{font-family:"Times New Roman",serif;font-weight:bold;font-size:large;color:gray;content:" “ ";vertical-align:-45%;line-height:0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-quote.quoted:after{font-family:"Times New Roman",serif;font-weight:bold;font-size:large;color:gray;content:" ” ";line-height:0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .left-aligned{text-align:left}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .right-aligned{text-align:right}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .center-aligned{text-align:center}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .quote-title,.mw-parser-output .quotebox .quotebox-quote{display:block}.mw-parser-output .quotebox cite{display:block;font-style:normal}@media screen and (max-width:640px){.mw-parser-output .quotebox{width:100%!important;margin:0 0 .8em!important;float:none!important}}</style><div class="quotebox pullquote floatright" style="width:300px; ;">
<blockquote class="quotebox-quote left-aligned" style="">
<p>The greatest value of a picture is when it forces us to notice what we never expected to see.
</p>
</blockquote>
<p><cite class="left-aligned" style=""><a href="/wiki/John_Tukey" title="John Tukey">John Tukey</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tukey1977_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tukey1977-11">[11]</a></sup></cite>
</p>
</div>
<p>Professor <a href="/wiki/Edward_Tufte" title="Edward Tufte">Edward Tufte</a> explained that users of information displays are executing particular <i>analytical tasks</i>  such as making comparisons. The <i>design principle</i> of the information graphic should support the analytical task.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> As William Cleveland and Robert McGill show, different graphical elements accomplish this more or less effectively. For example, dot plots and bar charts outperform pie charts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>In his 1983 book <i>The Visual Display of Quantitative Information</i>, <a href="/wiki/Edward_Tufte" title="Edward Tufte">Edward Tufte</a> defines 'graphical displays' and principles for effective graphical display in the following passage:
"Excellence in statistical graphics consists of complex ideas communicated with clarity, precision, and efficiency. Graphical displays should:
</p>
<ul><li>show the data</li>
<li>induce the viewer to think about the substance rather than about methodology, graphic design, the technology of graphic production, or something else</li>
<li>avoid distorting what the data has to say</li>
<li>present many numbers in a small space</li>
<li>make large data sets coherent</li>
<li>encourage the eye to compare different pieces of data</li>
<li>reveal the data at several levels of detail, from a broad overview to the fine structure</li>
<li>serve a reasonably clear purpose: description, exploration, tabulation, or decoration</li>
<li>be closely integrated with the statistical and verbal descriptions of a data set.</li></ul>
<p>Graphics <i>reveal</i> data.  Indeed graphics can be more precise and revealing than conventional statistical computations."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tufte1983_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tufte1983-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>For example, the Minard diagram shows the losses suffered by Napoleon's army in the 1812–1813 period. Six variables are plotted: the size of the army, its location on a two-dimensional surface (x and y), time, the direction of movement, and temperature. The line width illustrates a comparison (size of the army at points in time), while the temperature axis suggests a cause of the change in army size. This multivariate display on a two-dimensional surface tells a story that can be grasped immediately while identifying the source data to build credibility.  Tufte wrote in 1983 that: "It may well be the best statistical graphic ever drawn."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tufte1983_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Tufte1983-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>Not applying these principles may result in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Misleading_graphs" title="Misleading graphs">misleading graphs</a>, distorting the message, or supporting an erroneous conclusion. According to Tufte, <a href="/wiki/Chartjunk" title="Chartjunk">chartjunk</a> refers to the extraneous interior decoration of the graphic that does not enhance the message or gratuitous three-dimensional or perspective effects. Needlessly separating the explanatory key from the image itself, requiring the eye to travel back and forth from the image to the key, is a form of "administrative debris." The ratio of "data to ink" should be maximized, erasing non-data ink where feasible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tufte1983_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-Tufte1983-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Congressional_Budget_Office" title="Congressional Budget Office">Congressional Budget Office</a> summarized several best practices for graphical displays in a June 2014 presentation.  These included: a) Knowing your audience; b) Designing graphics that can stand alone outside the report's context; and c) Designing graphics that communicate the key messages in the report.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Quantitative_messages">Quantitative messages</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:392px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Total_Revenues_and_Outlays_as_Percent_GDP_2013.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="484" data-file-width="1025" decoding="async" height="184" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Total_Revenues_and_Outlays_as_Percent_GDP_2013.png/390px-Total_Revenues_and_Outlays_as_Percent_GDP_2013.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Total_Revenues_and_Outlays_as_Percent_GDP_2013.png/585px-Total_Revenues_and_Outlays_as_Percent_GDP_2013.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Total_Revenues_and_Outlays_as_Percent_GDP_2013.png/780px-Total_Revenues_and_Outlays_as_Percent_GDP_2013.png 2x" width="390"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Total_Revenues_and_Outlays_as_Percent_GDP_2013.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A time series illustrated with a line chart demonstrating trends in U.S. federal spending and revenue over time</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:332px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:U.S._Phillips_Curve_2000_to_2013.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="720" data-file-width="960" decoding="async" height="248" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/U.S._Phillips_Curve_2000_to_2013.png/330px-U.S._Phillips_Curve_2000_to_2013.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/U.S._Phillips_Curve_2000_to_2013.png/495px-U.S._Phillips_Curve_2000_to_2013.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/U.S._Phillips_Curve_2000_to_2013.png/660px-U.S._Phillips_Curve_2000_to_2013.png 2x" width="330"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:U.S._Phillips_Curve_2000_to_2013.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A scatterplot illustrating negative correlation between two variables (inflation and unemployment) measured at points in time</div></div></div>
<p>Author Stephen Few described eight types of quantitative messages that users may attempt to understand or communicate from a set of data and the associated graphs used to help communicate the message:
</p>
<ol><li>Time-series: A single variable is captured over a period of time, such as the unemployment rate over a 10-year period. A <a href="/wiki/Line_chart" title="Line chart">line chart</a> may be used to demonstrate the trend.</li>
<li>Ranking: Categorical subdivisions are ranked in ascending or descending order, such as a ranking of sales performance (the <i>measure</i>) by sales persons (the <i>category</i>, with each sales person a <i>categorical subdivision</i>) during a single period.  A <a href="/wiki/Bar_chart" title="Bar chart">bar chart</a> may be used to show the comparison across the sales persons.</li>
<li>Part-to-whole: Categorical subdivisions are measured as a ratio to the whole (i.e., a percentage out of 100%).  A <a href="/wiki/Pie_chart" title="Pie chart">pie chart</a> or bar chart can show the comparison of ratios, such as the market share represented by competitors in a market.</li>
<li>Deviation: Categorical subdivisions are compared against a reference, such as a comparison of actual vs. budget expenses for several departments of a business for a given time period.  A bar chart can show comparison of the actual versus the reference amount.</li>
<li>Frequency distribution: Shows the number of observations of a particular variable for given interval, such as the number of years in which the stock market return is between intervals such as 0-10%, 11-20%, etc. A <a href="/wiki/Histogram" title="Histogram">histogram</a>, a type of bar chart, may be used for this analysis. A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boxplot" title="Boxplot">boxplot</a> helps visualize key statistics about the distribution, such as median, quartiles, outliers, etc.</li>
<li>Correlation: Comparison between observations represented by two variables (X,Y) to determine if they tend to move in the same or opposite directions. For example, plotting unemployment (X) and inflation (Y) for a sample of months. A <a href="/wiki/Scatter_plot" title="Scatter plot">scatter plot</a> is typically used for this message.</li>
<li>Nominal comparison: Comparing categorical subdivisions in no particular order, such as the sales volume by product code. A bar chart may be used for this comparison.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Geography" title="Geography">Geographic</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geospatial" title="Geospatial">geospatial</a>: Comparison of a variable across a map or layout, such as the unemployment rate by state or the number of persons on the various floors of a building. A <a href="/wiki/Cartogram" title="Cartogram">cartogram</a> is a typical graphic used.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></li></ol>
<p>Analysts reviewing a set of data may consider whether some or all of the messages and graphic types above are applicable to their task and audience. The process of trial and error to identify meaningful relationships and messages in the data is part of <a href="/wiki/Exploratory_data_analysis" title="Exploratory data analysis">exploratory data analysis</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Visual_perception_and_data_visualization">Visual perception and data visualization</span></h3>
<p>A human can distinguish differences in line length, shape, orientation, distances, and color (hue) readily without significant processing effort; these are referred to as "<a href="/wiki/Pre-attentive_processing" title="Pre-attentive processing">pre-attentive attributes</a>".  For example, it may require significant time and effort ("attentive processing") to identify the number of times the digit "5" appears in a series of numbers; but if that digit is different in size, orientation, or color, instances of the digit can be noted quickly through pre-attentive processing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-perceptualedge.com_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-perceptualedge.com-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>Compelling graphics take advantage of pre-attentive processing and attributes and the relative strength of these attributes. For example, since humans can more easily process differences in line length than surface area, it may be more effective to use a bar chart (which takes advantage of line length to show comparison) rather than pie charts (which use surface area to show comparison).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-perceptualedge.com_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-perceptualedge.com-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span id="Human_perception.2Fcognition_and_data_visualization"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Human_perception/cognition_and_data_visualization">Human perception/cognition and data visualization</span></h4>
<p>Almost all data visualizations are created for human consumption. Knowledge of human perception and cognition is necessary when designing intuitive visualizations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-18">[18]</a></sup> Cognition refers to processes in human beings like perception, attention, learning, memory, thought, concept formation, reading, and problem solving.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Human visual processing is efficient in detecting changes and making comparisons between quantities, sizes, shapes and variations in lightness. When properties of symbolic data are mapped to visual properties, humans can browse through large amounts of data efficiently. It is estimated that 2/3 of the brain's neurons can be involved in visual processing. Proper visualization provides a different approach to show potential connections, relationships, etc. which are not as obvious in non-visualized quantitative data. Visualization can become a means of <a href="/wiki/Data_exploration" title="Data exploration">data exploration</a>.
</p><p>Studies have shown individuals used on average 19% less cognitive resources, and 4.5% better able to recall details when comparing data visualization with text.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1033289096">.mw-parser-output .hatnote{font-style:italic}.mw-parser-output div.hatnote{padding-left:1.6em;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .hatnote i{font-style:normal}.mw-parser-output .hatnote+link+.hatnote{margin-top:-0.5em}</style><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infographics#History" title="Infographics">Infographics § History</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Information_visualization#History" title="Information visualization">Information visualization § History</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:50_years_of_datavisulization_berengueres_own_work.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="193" data-file-width="508" decoding="async" height="84" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/50_years_of_datavisulization_berengueres_own_work.png/220px-50_years_of_datavisulization_berengueres_own_work.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/50_years_of_datavisulization_berengueres_own_work.png/330px-50_years_of_datavisulization_berengueres_own_work.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/50_years_of_datavisulization_berengueres_own_work.png/440px-50_years_of_datavisulization_berengueres_own_work.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:50_years_of_datavisulization_berengueres_own_work.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Selected milestones and inventions</div></div></div>
<p>There is no comprehensive 'history' of data visualization. There are no accounts that span the entire development of visual thinking and the visual representation of data, and which collate the contributions of disparate disciplines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Springer-Verlag_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Springer-Verlag-21">[21]</a></sup> Michael Friendly and Daniel J Denis of <a href="/wiki/York_University" title="York University">York University</a> are engaged in a project that attempts to provide a comprehensive history of visualization. Contrary to general belief, data visualization is not a modern development. Since prehistory, stellar data, or information such as location of stars were visualized on the walls of caves (such as those found in <a href="/wiki/Lascaux" title="Lascaux">Lascaux Cave</a> in Southern France) since the <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">Pleistocene</a> era.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WhitehouseIce00_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-WhitehouseIce00-22">[22]</a></sup> Physical artefacts such as Mesopotamian <a href="/wiki/History_of_ancient_numeral_systems#Clay_token" title="History of ancient numeral systems">clay tokens</a> (5500 BC), Inca <a href="/wiki/Quipu" title="Quipu">quipus</a> (2600 BC) and Marshall Islands <a href="/wiki/Marshall_Islands_stick_chart" title="Marshall Islands stick chart">stick charts</a> (n.d.) can also be considered as visualizing quantitative information.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dragicevic_2012_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dragicevic_2012-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>The first documented data visualization can be tracked back to 1160 B.C. with <a href="/wiki/Turin_Papyrus_Map" title="Turin Papyrus Map">Turin Papyrus Map</a> which accurately illustrates the distribution of geological resources and provides information about quarrying of those resources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Friendly_2001_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Friendly_2001-25">[25]</a></sup> Such maps can be categorized as <a href="/wiki/Thematic_map" title="Thematic map">thematic cartography</a>, which is a type of data visualization that presents and communicates specific data and information through a geographical illustration designed to show a particular theme connected with a specific geographic area. Earliest documented forms of data visualization were various thematic maps from different cultures and ideograms and hieroglyphs that provided and allowed interpretation of information illustrated. For example, <a href="/wiki/Linear_B" title="Linear B">Linear B</a> tablets of <a href="/wiki/Mycenae" title="Mycenae">Mycenae</a> provided a visualization of information regarding Late Bronze Age era trades in the Mediterranean. The idea of coordinates was used by ancient Egyptian surveyors in laying out towns, earthly and heavenly positions were located by something akin to latitude and longitude at least by 200 BC, and the map projection of a spherical earth into latitude and longitude by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Claudius_Ptolemy" title="Claudius Ptolemy">Claudius Ptolemy</a> [c.85–c. 165] in Alexandria would serve as reference standards until the 14th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Friendly_2001_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-Friendly_2001-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>The invention of paper and parchment allowed further development of visualizations throughout history. Figure shows a graph from the 10th or possibly 11th century that is intended to be an illustration of the planetary movement, used in an appendix of a textbook in monastery schools.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FUNKHOUSER_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-FUNKHOUSER-26">[26]</a></sup> The graph apparently was meant to represent a plot of the inclinations of the planetary orbits as a function of the time. For this purpose, the zone of the zodiac was represented on a plane with a horizontal line divided into thirty parts as the time or longitudinal axis. The vertical axis designates the width of the zodiac. The horizontal scale appears to have been chosen for each planet individually for the periods cannot be reconciled. The accompanying text refers only to the amplitudes. The curves are apparently not related in time.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:332px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mouvement_des_plan%C3%A8tes_au_cours_du_temps.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2786" data-file-width="4931" decoding="async" height="186" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Mouvement_des_plan%C3%A8tes_au_cours_du_temps.png/330px-Mouvement_des_plan%C3%A8tes_au_cours_du_temps.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Mouvement_des_plan%C3%A8tes_au_cours_du_temps.png/495px-Mouvement_des_plan%C3%A8tes_au_cours_du_temps.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Mouvement_des_plan%C3%A8tes_au_cours_du_temps.png/660px-Mouvement_des_plan%C3%A8tes_au_cours_du_temps.png 2x" width="330"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mouvement_des_plan%C3%A8tes_au_cours_du_temps.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Planetary movements</div></div></div>
<p>By the 16th century, techniques and instruments for precise observation and measurement of physical quantities, and geographic and celestial position were well-developed (for example, a “wall quadrant” constructed by <a href="/wiki/Tycho_Brahe" title="Tycho Brahe">Tycho Brahe</a> [1546–1601], covering an entire wall in his observatory). Particularly important were the development of triangulation and other methods to determine mapping locations accurately.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Springer-Verlag_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-Springer-Verlag-21">[21]</a></sup> Very early, the measure of time led scholars to develop innovative way of visualizing the data (e.g. Lorenz Codomann in 1596, Johannes Temporarius in 1596<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>).
</p><p>
French philosopher and mathematician <a href="/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Descartes" title="René Descartes">René Descartes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pierre_de_Fermat" title="Pierre de Fermat">Pierre de Fermat</a> developed analytic geometry and two-dimensional coordinate system which heavily influenced the practical methods of displaying and calculating values. Fermat and <a href="/wiki/Blaise_Pascal" title="Blaise Pascal">Blaise Pascal</a>'s work on statistics and probability theory laid the groundwork for what we now conceptualize as data.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Springer-Verlag_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-Springer-Verlag-21">[21]</a></sup> According to the Interaction Design Foundation, these developments allowed and helped William <a href="/wiki/William_Playfair" title="William Playfair">Playfair</a>, who saw potential for graphical communication of quantitative data, to generate and develop graphical methods of statistics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-18">[18]</a></sup> </p><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:332px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Playfair_TimeSeries.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1527" data-file-width="2067" decoding="async" height="244" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Playfair_TimeSeries.png/330px-Playfair_TimeSeries.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Playfair_TimeSeries.png/495px-Playfair_TimeSeries.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Playfair_TimeSeries.png/660px-Playfair_TimeSeries.png 2x" width="330"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Playfair_TimeSeries.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Playfair TimeSeries</div></div></div><p> In the second half of the 20th century, <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Bertin" title="Jacques Bertin">Jacques Bertin</a> used quantitative graphs to represent information "intuitively, clearly, accurately, and efficiently".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>John Tukey and Edward Tufte pushed the bounds of data visualization; Tukey with his new statistical approach of exploratory data analysis and Tufte with his book "The Visual Display of Quantitative Information" paved the way for refining data visualization techniques for more than statisticians. With the progression of technology came the progression of data visualization; starting with hand-drawn visualizations and evolving into more technical applications – including interactive designs leading to software visualization.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p><p>Programs like <a href="/wiki/SAS_(software)" title="SAS (software)">SAS</a>, <a href="/wiki/SOFA_Statistics" title="SOFA Statistics">SOFA</a>, <a href="/wiki/R_(programming_language)" title="R (programming language)">R</a>, <a href="/wiki/Minitab" title="Minitab">Minitab</a>, Cornerstone and more allow for data visualization in the field of statistics. Other data visualization applications, more focused and unique to individuals, programming languages such as <a href="/wiki/D3.js" title="D3.js">D3</a>, <a href="/wiki/Python_(programming_language)" title="Python (programming language)">Python</a> and <a href="/wiki/JavaScript" title="JavaScript">JavaScript</a> help to make the visualization of quantitative data a possibility.  Private schools have also developed programs to meet the demand for learning data visualization and associated programming libraries, including free programs like <a href="/wiki/The_Data_Incubator" title="The Data Incubator">The Data Incubator</a> or paid programs like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/General_Assembly" title="General Assembly">General Assembly</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>Beginning with the symposium "Data to Discovery" in 2013, ArtCenter College of Design, Caltech and JPL in Pasadena have run an annual program on interactive data visualization.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> The program asks: How can interactive data visualization help scientists and engineers explore their data more effectively? How can computing, design, and design thinking help maximize research results? What methodologies are most effective for leveraging knowledge from these fields? By encoding relational information with appropriate visual and interactive characteristics to help interrogate, and ultimately gain new insight into data, the program develops new interdisciplinary approaches to complex science problems, combining design thinking and the latest methods from computing, user-centered design, interaction design and 3D graphics.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">Terminology</span></h2>
<p>Data visualization involves specific terminology, some of which is derived from statistics. For example, author Stephen Few defines two types of data, which are used in combination to support a meaningful analysis or visualization:
</p>
<ul><li>Categorical: Represent groups of objects with a particular characteristic. Categorical variables can either be nominal or ordinal. Nominal variables for example gender have no order between them and are thus nominal. Ordinal variables are categories with an order, for sample recording the age group someone falls into.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-31">[31]</a></sup></li>
<li>Quantitative: Represent measurements, such as the height of a person or the temperature of an environment. Quantitative variables can either be <a href="/wiki/Continuous_or_discrete_variable" title="Continuous or discrete variable">continuous or discrete</a>. Continuous variables capture the idea that measurements can always be made more precisely. While discrete variables have only a finite number of possibilities, such as a count of some outcomes or an age measured in whole years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-31">[31]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>The distinction between quantitative and categorical variables is important because the two types require different methods of visualization.
</p><p>Two primary types of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Information_graphics" title="Information graphics">information displays</a> are tables and graphs.
</p>
<ul><li>A <i>table</i> contains quantitative data organized into rows and columns with categorical labels. It is primarily used to look up specific values. In the example above, the table might have categorical column labels representing the name (a <i>qualitative variable</i>) and age (a <i>quantitative variable</i>), with each row of data representing one person (the sampled <i>experimental unit</i> or <i>category subdivision</i>).</li>
<li>A <i>graph</i> is primarily used to show relationships among data and portrays values encoded as <i>visual objects</i> (e.g., lines, bars, or points). Numerical values are displayed within an area delineated by one or more <i>axes</i>. These axes provide <i>scales</i> (quantitative and categorical) used to label and assign values to the visual objects. Many graphs are also referred to as <i>charts</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>Eppler and Lengler have developed the "Periodic Table of Visualization Methods," an interactive chart displaying various data visualization methods. It includes six types of data visualization methods: data, information, concept, strategy, metaphor and compound.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Interactivity">Interactivity</span></h2>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interactive_visualization" title="Interactive visualization">Interactive visualization</a></div>
<p><b>Interactive data visualization</b>  enables direct actions on a graphical <a href="/wiki/Plot_(graphics)" title="Plot (graphics)">plot</a> to change elements and link between multiple plots.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p><p>Interactive data visualization has been a pursuit of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Statisticians" title="Statisticians">statisticians</a> since the late 1960s. Examples of the developments can be found on the <a href="/wiki/American_Statistical_Association" title="American Statistical Association">American Statistical Association</a> video lending library.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p><p>Common interactions include:
</p>
<ul><li><b><a href="/wiki/Brushing_and_linking" title="Brushing and linking">Brushing</a></b>: works by using the <a href="/wiki/Computer_mouse" title="Computer mouse">mouse</a> to control a paintbrush, directly changing the color or glyph of elements of a plot. The paintbrush is sometimes a pointer and sometimes works by drawing an outline of sorts around points; the outline is sometimes irregularly shaped, like a lasso. Brushing is most commonly used when multiple plots are visible and some linking mechanism exists between the plots. There are several different conceptual models for brushing and a number of common linking mechanisms. Brushing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scatterplots" title="Scatterplots">scatterplots</a> can be a transient operation in which points in the active plot only retain their new characteristics. At the same time, they are enclosed or intersected by the brush, or it can be a persistent operation, so that points retain their new appearance after the brush has been moved away. Transient brushing is usually chosen for linked brushing, as we have just described.</li>
<li><b>Painting</b>: Persistent brushing is useful when we want to group the points into clusters and then proceed to use other operations, such as the tour, to compare the groups. It is becoming common terminology to call the persistent operation painting,</li>
<li><b>Identification</b>: which could also be called labeling or label brushing, is another plot manipulation that can be linked.  Bringing the cursor near a point or edge in a scatterplot, or a bar in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barchart" title="Barchart">barchart</a>, causes a label to appear that identifies the plot element. It is widely available in many interactive graphics, and is sometimes called mouseover.</li>
<li><b>Scaling</b>: maps the data onto the window, and changes  in the area of the.  mapping function help us learn different things from the same plot.  Scaling is commonly used to zoom in on crowded regions of a scatterplot, and it can also be used to change the aspect ratio of a plot, to reveal different features of the data.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Brushing_and_linking" title="Brushing and linking">Linking</a></b>: connects elements selected in one plot with elements in another plot. The simplest kind of linking, one-to-one, where both plots show different projections of the same data, and a point in one plot corresponds to exactly one point in the other.  When using area plots, brushing any part of an area has the same effect as brushing it all and is equivalent to selecting all cases in the corresponding category.  Even when some plot elements represent more than one case, the underlying linking rule still links one case in one plot to the same case in other plots.  Linking can also be by categorical variable, such as by a subject id, so that all data values corresponding to that subject are highlighted, in all the visible plots.</li></ul>

<p>Within The Harvard Business Review, Scott Berinato developed a framework to approach data visualisation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-:2-41">[41]</a></sup> To start thinking visually, users must consider two questions; 1) What you have and 2) what you’re doing. The first step is identifying what data you want visualised. It is data-driven like profit over the past ten years or a conceptual idea like how a specific organisation is structured. Once this question is answered one can then focus on whether they are trying to communicate information (declarative visualisation) or trying to figure something out (exploratory visualisation). Scott Berinato combines these questions to give four types of visual communication that each have their own goals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-:2-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p><p>These four types of visual communication are as follows;
</p>
<ul><li>idea illustration (conceptual &amp; declarative).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_41-2"><a href="#cite_note-:2-41">[41]</a></sup>
<ul><li>Used to teach, explain and/or simply concepts. For example, organisation charts and decision trees.</li></ul></li>
<li>idea generation (conceptual &amp; exploratory).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_41-3"><a href="#cite_note-:2-41">[41]</a></sup>
<ul><li>Used to discover, innovate and solve problems. For example, a whiteboard after a brainstorming session.</li></ul></li>
<li>visual discovery (data-driven &amp; exploratory).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_41-4"><a href="#cite_note-:2-41">[41]</a></sup>
<ul><li>Used to spot trends and make sense of data. This type of visual is more common with large and complex data where the dataset is somewhat unknown and the task is open-ended.</li></ul></li>
<li>everyday data-visualisation (data-driven &amp; declarative).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_41-5"><a href="#cite_note-:2-41">[41]</a></sup>
<ul><li>The most common and simple type of visualisation used for affirming and setting context. For example, a line graph of GDP over time.</li></ul></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Data_presentation_architecture">Data presentation architecture</span></h2>

<p><b>Data presentation architecture</b> (<b>DPA</b>) is a skill-set that seeks to identify, locate, manipulate, format and present data in such a way as to optimally communicate meaning and proper knowledge.
</p><p>Historically, the term <i>data presentation architecture</i> is attributed to Kelly Lautt:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[a]</a></sup> "Data Presentation Architecture (DPA) is a rarely applied skill set critical for the success and value of <a href="/wiki/Business_intelligence" title="Business intelligence">Business Intelligence</a>. Data presentation architecture weds the science of numbers, data and statistics in <a href="/wiki/Information_discovery" title="Information discovery">discovering valuable information</a> from data and making it usable, relevant and actionable with the arts of data visualization, communications, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organizational_psychology" title="Organizational psychology">organizational psychology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Change_management" title="Change management">change management</a> in order to provide business intelligence solutions with the data scope, delivery timing, format and visualizations that will most effectively support and drive operational, tactical and strategic behaviour toward understood business (or organizational) goals. DPA is neither an IT nor a business skill set but exists as a separate field of expertise. Often confused with data visualization, data presentation architecture is a much broader skill set that includes determining what data on what schedule and in what exact format is to be presented, not just the best way to present data that has already been chosen. Data visualization skills are one element of DPA."
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Objectives">Objectives</span></h3>
<p>DPA has two main objectives:
</p>
<ul><li>To use data to provide knowledge in the most efficient manner possible (minimize noise, complexity, and unnecessary data or detail given each audience's needs and roles)</li>
<li>To use data to provide knowledge in the most effective manner possible  (provide relevant, timely and complete data to each audience member in a clear and understandable manner that conveys important meaning, is actionable and can affect understanding, behavior and decisions)</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Scope">Scope</span></h3>
<p>With the above objectives in mind, the actual work of data presentation architecture consists of:
</p>
<ul><li>Creating effective delivery mechanisms for each audience member depending on their role, tasks, locations and access to technology</li>
<li>Defining important meaning (relevant knowledge) that is needed by each audience member in each context</li>
<li>Determining the required periodicity of data updates (the currency of the data)</li>
<li>Determining the right timing for data presentation (when and how often the user needs to see the data)</li>
<li>Finding the right data (subject area, historical reach, breadth, level of detail, etc.)</li>
<li>Utilizing appropriate analysis, grouping, visualization, and other presentation formats</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Related_fields">Related fields</span></h3>
<p>DPA work shares commonalities with several other fields, including:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Business_analysis" title="Business analysis">Business analysis</a> in determining business goals, collecting requirements, mapping processes.</li>
<li>Business process improvement in that its goal is to improve and streamline actions and decisions in furtherance of business goals</li>
<li>Data visualization in that it uses well-established theories of visualization to add or highlight meaning or importance in data presentation.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Information_architecture" title="Information architecture">Information architecture</a>, but information architecture's focus is on <a href="/wiki/Unstructured_data" title="Unstructured data">unstructured data</a> and therefore excludes both analysis (in the statistical/data sense) and direct transformation of the actual content (data, for DPA) into new entities and combinations.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Human%E2%80%93computer_interaction" title="Human–computer interaction">HCI</a> and <a href="/wiki/Interaction_design" title="Interaction design">interaction design</a>, since many of the principles in how to design interactive data visualisation have been developed cross-disciplinary with HCI.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Visual_journalism" title="Visual journalism">Visual journalism</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Data-driven_journalism" title="Data-driven journalism">data-driven journalism</a> or <a href="/wiki/Data_journalism" title="Data journalism">data journalism</a>: Visual journalism is concerned with all types of graphic facilitation of the telling of news stories, and data-driven and data journalism are not necessarily told with data visualisation. Nevertheless, the field of journalism is at the forefront in developing new data visualisations to communicate data.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Graphic_design" title="Graphic design">Graphic design</a>, conveying information through styling, typography, position, and other aesthetic concerns.</li></ul>
